Global Deforestation Trends
Forests cover nearly a third of the world’s land area, but this coverage is rapidly shrinking. Explore the factors driving deforestation, from agricultural expansion to logging, and discover the impact of these changes on our environment.

Global Percentage of Forest Area

Forests cover nearly one-third of the planet’s land area, but this coverage is unevenly distributed. Russia holds the largest share of forested land, with over 800 million hectares, followed by other nations like Brazil, Canada, and the United States, which each contribute significantly to global forest resources.

Brazil, with its vast Amazon rainforest, is the only country besides Russia with more than 10% of the world’s total forest area, underscoring its critical role in biodiversity and carbon storage.

Despite the importance of these forests, human activities are reshaping the landscape. Expansion of agriculture, urbanization, and logging have led to a sharp decline in global forest cover, threatening ecosystems and contributing to climate change. As the demand for land grows, preserving these natural resources becomes an even greater challenge.

Global Forest Conversion

Forest areas are constantly changing, with some countries achieving positive net growth through reforestation efforts, while others face substantial losses due to logging, agriculture, and urban expansion. This map reveals where forest areas are increasing and where they’re dwindling.

Each year, the world loses around 5 million hectares of forest, primarily in tropical regions, where about 95% of deforestation occurs. The main driver? Agriculture—especially the conversion of forests into farmland and pastures for livestock and crop production. These changes challenge global efforts to sustain forest ecosystems and biodiversity.

Drivers of Forest Loss in Brazil

In recent decades, Brazil has increased its use of land for soy production and pasture, driven largely by global demand for soybeans and beef. But at what cost to the Amazon rainforest?

The area chart shows forest loss over time, broken down by key causes. Among these, pasture expansion has had the most significant impact on Brazil’s forests, particularly in the Amazon, where vast areas of rainforest have been converted to grazing land.

In 2006, Brazil introduced the Soy Moratorium, a voluntary agreement by major soy producers to reduce deforestation linked to soy cultivation. This intervention has been largely successful, as evidenced by a significant decline in forest loss due to commercial crops following its implementation.

Palm Oil Production in South East Asia

Palm oil production is concentrated in a few key countries, with Indonesia and Malaysia dominating the global market. These two nations alone account for 63% of the world's palm oil land use, despite producing 84% of global output. Their high efficiency and yields make them leaders in this industry.

In total, more than 300 million hectares of global land are used for oil crops, but only 6% is dedicated to palm oil. Remarkably, this small percentage of land produces over 36% of the world's vegetable oil, highlighting palm oil's high productivity.

Soy Production and Usages

Soybeans have become an essential crop worldwide due to their versatile applications. The chart shows three main categories: Processed products, Human food, and Animal feed.

Processed products, which make up the largest portion of soy demand, include animal feed, soybean oil, and biofuels. This category meets around 70% of global soy demand. Human food includes soy-based foods consumed directly, while Animal feed represents soybeans used directly for livestock.

The rapid growth in global meat production over the past five decades, especially in poultry, has significantly increased demand for soy, making it a vital component of animal feed worldwide.
